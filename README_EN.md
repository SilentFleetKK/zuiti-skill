## See it work

Every reply below is a finished message you can copy and send — not advice about what you "could" say. The three tiers are a ladder of stance, not three politeness levels of the same sentence:

- **Composed** — lands it gracefully, defuses the tension, and doesn't take the bait.
- **Velvet-and-needle** — sounds perfectly courteous, but plants exactly one barb inside a completely reasonable request, so the other side can neither swallow it nor call it out.
- **Flip-the-table** — names the game out loud, puts your terms on the record, keeps it short and direct — firm, but never a slur, an insult, or a threat.

### The friend who won't pay you back

> **Friend** (borrowed money, three months of silence)
> 最近手头是不是宽裕点了？上次那个事儿……你懂的哈，不急不急。
> *"Things looking a little easier for you lately? That thing from a while back… you know the one. No rush, no rush."*

**zuiti:**

🎭 **Subtext**
He owes *you* money, yet he flips it to "are *you* flush now?" — fishing to see whether you'll bring it up yourself, or hinting that "if you've got money, don't chase me." "No rush, no rush" is the tell: someone who genuinely wasn't in a rush wouldn't send this text at all.
**Misread risk: low.** He raised "that thing from a while back" on his own, but deliberately left it vague — that fog is the move.

💬 **Three tiers**

**[Composed]**
Ha, still muddling along the same as ever~ Oh, and since it came up — whenever that one's convenient for you, just sort it out then, no rush, just keep it on your radar for me.

**[Velvet-and-needle]**
Things are a bit tight on my end, honestly~ Since you mentioned "that thing," I've been keeping track of it too. Any chance this month works? Doesn't have to be all at once — in parts is fine. Let's just get it closed out, easier on both of us.

**[Flip-the-table]**
"Flush" would be a stretch, honestly. Since you brought up that thing from before, let's just say it plainly: that money's been out three months now — when can you get it back to me? Put a date on it so I know where things stand. I'm not chasing you; the numbers just have to add up, and that's what keeps a friendship going long-term.

🧭 **Long game**
A vague debt is what quietly kills a friendship. Each time, calmly put "let's set a date" on the table — no fighting, no dredging up old grievances — turn it into a thing with a deadline. That's what actually protects the relationship.

📌 **Which to send**
Lead with **[Velvet-and-needle]**: it drags the money back into the open *and* hands him a way to pay plus a face-saving off-ramp.

### The passive-aggressive manager

*(Western-context example — a Friday-evening Slack from a manager who's very good at making "no big deal" mean "your weekend is gone.")*

> **Manager** (Slack, 6:47 p.m. Friday)
> Hey — quick one! I'm presenting to leadership Monday AM. Could you just polish the deck over the weekend? Doesn't need to be perfect, you're fast, should be an hour or two tops 🙏

**zuiti:**

🎭 **Subtext**
"Just," "doesn't need to be perfect," and "an hour or two" aren't kindness — they're a pre-emptive re-labeling of the job as trivial, so you feel too awkward to say no *and* too awkward to ask what it's worth. "You're fast" is the sugar coating. The core: give up your weekend, do it quickly, expect nothing for it.
**Misread risk: low.** The timestamp, the Monday deadline, and the "hour or two" together add up to a clear claim on your weekend.

💬 **Three tiers**

**[Composed]**
Happy to take a pass this weekend. So I nail it in one go rather than guess — which slides matter most, and what's the one thing leadership will zero in on? I'll get those tight so it's ready to present Monday morning.

**[Velvet-and-needle]**
Sure, I can pick this up. I wouldn't promise "an hour or two," though — for a leadership deck I'd rather not build to a "doesn't need to be perfect" bar. I'll block time and get you the final Sunday evening. I'll log the weekend hours and take the time back next week 👍

**[Flip-the-table]**
Happy to help make this land. Two things worth naming up front, though. One: a leadership deck built "quick, doesn't need to be perfect" is how it goes sideways in the room — if it does, whose call was that? Two: I'll cover the weekend, but tell me whether it's comp time or logged overtime, and I'll start the moment that's settled.

🧭 **Long game**
"No big deal / super quick / just polish it" is the universal opener for unpaid work — the lighter the ask sounds, the less anyone feels they owe you for it. From now on, whenever a task gets called "quick" or "simple," quote the real scope back before you accept. Price your time every time, and they'll start weighing it before they toss it over.

📌 **Which to send**
If you still need the relationship intact, **[Velvet-and-needle]** is safest: you take the work, and you quietly put "comp time" and the real workload on the table in the same breath.

### The client who wants version one back

> **Client** (after five rounds of revisions)
> 我们领导看了，还是觉得第一版顺眼，改回第一版吧。尽快哈，上线时间不变。
> *"Leadership took a look and still likes version one best — let's revert to version one. ASAP please. Launch date stays the same."*

**zuiti:**

🎭 **Subtext**
Five rounds only to land back at the start means nobody on their side can make a call, and they're expecting *you* to eat the cost of that. "Launch date stays the same" is the real sting: the time *their* indecision burned is being quietly deducted from *your* schedule.
**Misread risk: low.** The revision history is documented; the ask to revert with an unchanged deadline is explicit.

💬 **Three tiers**

**[Composed]**
Sure, we can revert to version one. To keep the launch date, I'll need leadership's written sign-off that v1 is final so we're not re-opening it again — send that over and I'll get straight to it.

**[Velvet-and-needle]**
Happy to revert to version one. Since we've gone five rounds, let me quickly line two things up: I'll pull together an hours summary across those rounds so we're on the same page about scope, and once leadership confirms v1 in writing as locked, I'll start today and hold the launch date.

**[Flip-the-table]**
We can revert to version one, but two things up front. One: all five rounds ran on your team's direction, and those hours are a real cost — I'll put together an itemized summary and we should talk through how that's settled. Two: "launch date stays the same" holds *only* if this version is locked — leadership confirms v1 in writing, no more changes after. Settle both and I start today.

🧭 **Long game**
When a contract has no cap on revision rounds, indecision on their side becomes free for them and expensive for you. If you're not being consistently drained, use **[Velvet-and-needle]** to get the written lock. If you've already been ground down round after round, send **[Flip-the-table]** and make the rules stick.

📌 **Which to send**
No revision cap in the contract? Use **[Velvet-and-needle]** first to secure the sign-off. Already burned repeatedly? Send **[Flip-the-table]** and set the boundary.

### Mom, and the marriage question

*(Cultural note: in many Chinese families, parents actively arranging blind dates — and framing your single status as their unfinished "worry" — is a common, loaded pressure. The point here is that not every hard message is a trap: sometimes the pressure is real love, badly aimed, and the honest read says so.)*

> **Mom**
> 王阿姨给你介绍了个男孩子，条件真的很好，见一面怎么了？妈不图你什么，就这一个心事。你别嫌我们烦，我跟你爸年纪都上来了。
> *"Auntie Wang has a young man for you to meet — he's really a great match, what's the harm in one meeting? Mom doesn't want anything from you, this is my one worry. Don't think we're pestering you; your dad and I aren't getting any younger."*

**zuiti:**

🎭 **Subtext**
There's no scheme here. The anxiety is real, and so is the love. What she wants isn't for you to marry tomorrow — it's the reassurance that "someone's on top of this." Don't brace against it like an attack; the harder you push back, the more anxious she gets.

💬 **The one to send: [Velvet-and-needle]**

Mom, I can meet him. But I want to be clear about one thing: it's not that I'm not taking it seriously — it's that I want to find the right person, not just settle. I understand this weighs on you and Dad, but please don't carry it as *your* burden — I'm genuinely doing well, and that alone should be the thing that lets you relax.

📌 **Which to send**
In close relationships, drop the "winning" posture. Catch the feeling first, then state the boundary clearly — it almost always works better than pushing straight back.

## What it actually does

A plain rewording tool just makes you sound polite. zuiti does four things instead:

| Module | What it's for | What you get |
|---|---|---|
| Subtext translation | See whether they're applying pressure, testing you, dodging blame, playing the victim, or genuinely asking for help | The emotion lands first, so you're not swept along by the tactic |
| Misread risk | Flags how sure the read is, so you don't over-interpret an ordinary message | A brake to go with the catharsis |
| Three tiers | Composed / Velvet-and-needle / Flip-the-table | Pick by relationship and consequences, instead of white-knuckling it live |
| Which to send | Says which tier fits your actual situation | One more layer of reality-check before you hit send |

zuiti is not an insult machine. Its goal is to leave you **calmer, better-bounded, and standing on firmer ground.**

## Install

Open the agent you already use (Claude Code / Codex / Cursor / OpenClaw, etc.) and tell it:

```text
Install this skill for me: https://github.com/SilentFleetKK/zuiti-skill
```

Or use the universal CLI installer:

```bash
npx skills add SilentFleetKK/zuiti-skill
```

It detects your runtime and drops the skill into the right directory. To target a specific environment, add `-a claude-code` / `-a codex` / `-a cursor`, etc.

Even if your runtime doesn't support auto-loading, you can paste the contents of `SKILL.md` straight into the conversation — it's just markdown.

Manual install paths: see [INSTALL.md](INSTALL.md).

## How to use

Once it's installed, whenever a message stumps you, just paste it to your agent:

```text
> Help me answer this: [paste the message they sent you]
> What does this even mean, and how do I reply: [message]
> zuiti, [message]
```

You get the same structured output every time:

1. **Subtext** — what they're saying on the surface, and what they're actually trying to push through.
2. **Misread risk** — how confident that read is, and where it might just be a misunderstanding.
3. **Three tiers** — Composed / Velvet-and-needle / Flip-the-table, each ready to copy and send.
4. **Long game** — how to handle this relationship or this kind of message with less friction next time.
5. **Which to send** — the tier that fits your situation right now, and why.

## Your own mouthpiece

Out of the box, zuiti speaks in a "polished and proper" register. You can also have it learn *your* voice, so every reply reads like something you actually typed — not something with a whiff of AI on it.

```text
> Learn how I talk — here are my usual chats: [paste / upload]
> Distill my style and build me my own mouthpiece
```

It pulls out:

- Your pet phrases, go-to words, and filler particles
- Sentence length, punctuation, and how you break up a thought
- How you use emoji and reactions
- Your emotional baseline: warm, distant, steady, playful, sharp
- Your calibration: which tier you actually reach for, and which one you keep in reserve

The generated persona skill is saved to `zuiti-personas/{name}/SKILL.md`. Template at [`personas/template/SKILL.md`](personas/template/SKILL.md); the distillation method is in [`references/个人口吻蒸馏模板.md`](references/个人口吻蒸馏模板.md).

## Safety

zuiti's hardest setting is: *make the case airtight, hold your boundary, and don't burn the bridge.*

- No profanity, no personal attacks, no threats, no fabrication.
- No encouraging revenge, harassment, illegal acts, or anything that creates real-world risk.
- It won't decide for you whether to grit your teeth or blow it up — it lays out the options and their consequences.
- For intimate relationships, family, mental-health crises, or any risk of violence, it prioritizes real-world safety over a clever line.
